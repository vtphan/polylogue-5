# Facet Inventory

This document defines the specific facet inventory for the Polylogue framework. It operationalizes the hidden layer described in the conceptual framework (`polylogue-v5-4.md`, Section 3) — instantiating the structural properties of facets with a concrete, revisable set of eleven dimensions across three lenses.

This inventory is a design choice, not a theoretical commitment. The structural properties (facets are continuous dimensions, have cross-lens visibility, map many-to-many to explanatory variables, and pivot between evaluation and explanation) are framework-level claims meant to be stable. The specific facets listed here — their number, names, groupings, and quality range descriptions — are meant to be validated and revised through classroom use.

**Audiences:** Pipeline designers (generation targets), evaluator agents (assessment criteria), teachers (scaffolding vocabulary). Students never see this document or its terminology.

---

## Reading Guide

Each facet is defined by four components:

- **Definition** — what dimension of reasoning quality it captures
- **Quality range** — what strong and weak look like (anchor points on a continuous dimension, not binary states)
- **Cross-lens visibility** — which other lens(es) can reveal this facet, and how a student looking through that lens might articulate it
- **Explanatory connections** — which cognitive patterns and social dynamics commonly produce weakness on this facet (illustrative co-occurrences for pipeline design, not an exhaustive mapping; students may offer valid explanations not listed here)

## Evidence Lens

### Source Credibility

The reliability and trustworthiness of the sources cited.

| Strong | Weak |
|---|---|
| Sources are authoritative, independent, and appropriate for the claim | Sources are unreliable, self-interested, or inappropriate for the type of claim being made |

**Cross-lens visibility:** Logic — a student looking through Logic may say "their whole argument falls apart because that fact they started with isn't even true," noticing that a conclusion built on an unreliable premise doesn't hold, without framing it as an evidence problem.

**Explanatory connections:** Uncritical acceptance (didn't question the source), authority deference (group treated a confident citation as settled).

### Source Diversity

The range and independence of evidence brought to bear.

| Strong | Weak |
|---|---|
| Multiple independent sources, including sources that challenge the claim | Single source, or multiple sources that trace to the same origin |

**Cross-lens visibility:** Scope — a student looking through Scope may notice that all evidence comes from one type of source as a narrowness problem rather than an evidence problem.

**Explanatory connections:** Confirmation bias (sought only agreeing sources), tunnel vision (fixated on first source found), groupthink (group accepted one source and moved on).

### Relevance

How well the evidence connects to the specific claim being made.

| Strong | Weak |
|---|---|
| Evidence directly supports the claim as stated, at the scope being claimed | Evidence is tangentially related, supports a different claim, or supports a narrower version of the claim |

**Cross-lens visibility:** Logic — a student looking through Logic may say "that example doesn't have anything to do with what they're trying to prove," seeing the relevance failure as an inferential gap rather than an evidence problem. This is one of the highest-overlap facets in the inventory.

**Explanatory connections:** Overgeneralization (evidence for a narrow claim applied broadly), confirmation bias (treated loosely related evidence as supporting because it felt aligned).

### Sufficiency

Whether the quantity and weight of evidence matches the scope of the conclusion.

| Strong | Weak |
|---|---|
| Evidence is proportional to the claim — bigger claims backed by more and stronger evidence | Ranges from a large conclusion resting on thin evidence (one anecdote, one statistic) to bare assertion — a claim offered with no supporting evidence at all |

**Cross-lens visibility:** Scope — a student may see this as "they drew a big conclusion from a small amount" (sufficiency) or "they didn't consider enough to justify something that broad" (scope). Logic — the gap between evidence weight and conclusion strength is also an inferential problem.

**Explanatory connections:** Overgeneralization (most direct), false certainty (felt confident despite thin evidence), conformity (group accepted thin evidence without challenge).

## Logic Lens

### Inferential Validity

Whether the conclusion actually follows from the premises offered.

| Strong | Weak |
|---|---|
| The conclusion is a reasonable inference from what was stated | The conclusion doesn't follow — it's a leap, a non sequitur, or a structural error |

**Cross-lens visibility:** Rarely visible through other lenses. This is the most Logic-specific facet — when the inferential step itself is broken, it's almost always perceived as a logic problem. This makes it the least productive facet for generating perspectival diversity; students will tend to converge.

**Explanatory connections:** False cause (assumed causation from correlation or sequence), black-and-white thinking (collapsed a nuanced inference into a binary).

**Pipeline note:** Causal errors — asserting causation from correlation, temporal sequence, or mere association — are a high-value manifestation of weak inferential validity for this age group. They are common in discussions about science, policy, and everyday reasoning, and accessible to 6th graders. The pipeline can target "inferential validity, specifically causal reasoning" as a generation instruction without causal reasoning needing its own facet.

### Internal Consistency

Whether the claims made within the discussion contradict each other.

| Strong | Weak |
|---|---|
| Claims build on and reinforce each other — positions stated early remain consistent with conclusions drawn later, and the group notices and resolves apparent tensions | Claims contradict — a character (or the group) asserts things that can't both be true |

**Cross-lens visibility:** Evidence — a student might notice contradictory evidence as an evidence problem ("they cited two things that say opposite things") rather than a logical one.

**Explanatory connections:** Tunnel vision (focused so narrowly on the current point that they forgot what they said earlier), conflict avoidance (group didn't flag the contradiction to avoid confrontation), conformity (character agreed with contradictory positions to stay aligned with different group members).

### Reasoning Completeness

Whether the steps between premises and conclusion are explicit or whether critical steps are missing.

| Strong | Weak |
|---|---|
| The reasoning chain is traceable — you can follow how they got from evidence to conclusion | Critical steps are skipped — the conclusion appears with a gap where the reasoning should be |

**Cross-lens visibility:** Evidence — a student looking through Evidence may say "they jumped to the conclusion without showing how the evidence supports it," seeing a missing reasoning step as missing evidential support.

**Explanatory connections:** False certainty (the conclusion felt obvious so they didn't articulate the steps), groupthink (the group shared enough assumptions that the missing steps went unnoticed), authority deference (the confident person's leap went unchallenged).

## Scope Lens

### Perspective Breadth

The range of stakeholder viewpoints, experiences, or positions considered.

| Strong | Weak |
|---|---|
| Multiple relevant perspectives are considered, including those that challenge the group's position | Only one perspective is represented, or only perspectives that agree with the conclusion |

**Cross-lens visibility:** Evidence — a student may notice missing perspectives as missing evidence ("they didn't look at evidence from people who disagree"). This overlap is common and productive.

**Explanatory connections:** Egocentric thinking (only considered perspectives like their own), confirmation bias (ignored perspectives that challenged their view), groupthink (the group converged on one viewpoint and stopped looking), conflict avoidance (a dissenting perspective was raised but dropped).

### Consequence Consideration

Whether downstream effects, second-order implications, and unintended outcomes are examined.

| Strong | Weak |
|---|---|
| The discussion considers what would happen if their conclusion were acted on — including unintended effects | The discussion stops at the conclusion without examining its implications |

**Cross-lens visibility:** Logic — a student looking through Logic may say "they proved it's good in one way but didn't check if it causes other problems," seeing the failure to consider consequences as an incomplete argument.

**Explanatory connections:** Tunnel vision (focused on the immediate conclusion, didn't look further), black-and-white thinking (treated the solution as purely good or purely bad), groupthink (the group was satisfied with the conclusion and didn't stress-test it).

### Condition Sensitivity

Whether limitations, exceptions, and boundary conditions are acknowledged.

| Strong | Weak |
|---|---|
| The discussion qualifies its conclusions — "this works under these conditions," "this doesn't apply when..." | The conclusion is stated as universal or absolute, with no conditions or exceptions |

**Cross-lens visibility:** Logic — a student looking through Logic may say "they said this would always work, but their own reasons only showed it works in some cases," seeing the unconditional conclusion from conditional premises as an inferential problem. Evidence — a student may say "the evidence they gave was only about one situation, so how do they know it works everywhere?" framing it as an evidence gap.

**Explanatory connections:** Overgeneralization (most direct), false certainty (felt confident enough to state without qualification), conformity (the group settled on a clean conclusion and no one complicated it).

### Counter-Argument Engagement

Whether opposing positions are addressed, rebutted, or incorporated rather than ignored.

| Strong | Weak |
|---|---|
| Opposing positions are addressed — engaged with, rebutted with reasoning, or acknowledged as limitations | Opposing positions are absent or dismissed without reasoning |

**Cross-lens visibility:** Evidence — a student looking through Evidence may say "they just said the other side was wrong without showing why," seeing the dismissal as unsupported. Logic — a student looking through Logic may say "they twisted what the other person said and then argued against that instead," seeing a straw man as a logical failure.

**Explanatory connections:** Confirmation bias (ignored arguments against their position), conflict avoidance (dissent was dropped rather than pursued), authority deference (the counter-argument was dismissed by the most confident member and the group followed), groupthink (the group treated agreement as success).

## Cross-Lens Visibility Map

This summarizes which facets are visible from lenses other than their primary home. Facets with more cross-lens connections are more likely to produce perspectival diversity — two students looking through different lenses seeing the same underlying feature and articulating it differently.

| Facet | Primary Lens | Also Visible Through |
|---|---|---|
| Source credibility | Evidence | Logic |
| Source diversity | Evidence | Scope |
| Relevance | Evidence | Logic |
| Sufficiency | Evidence | Scope, Logic |
| Inferential validity | Logic | — (convergent — students will tend to agree) |
| Internal consistency | Logic | Evidence |
| Reasoning completeness | Logic | Evidence |
| Perspective breadth | Scope | Evidence |
| Consequence consideration | Scope | Logic |
| Condition sensitivity | Scope | Logic, Evidence |
| Counter-argument engagement | Scope | Evidence, Logic |

**Observations:**

**Inferential validity** is the only facet with no cross-lens visibility. When the inference itself is broken, it's almost always seen as a logic problem. This makes it the least productive facet for perspectival diversity — students will tend to converge. It is useful for teaching Logic specifically but less productive for the perspectival model.

**The highest-overlap facets** are relevance, sufficiency, condition sensitivity, and counter-argument engagement. These sit at the boundaries between lenses and are the most likely to produce the kind of perspectival diversity the framework depends on — two students looking through different lenses, seeing the same underlying feature, articulating it differently. These are strong candidates for early scenarios when rich peer exchange is the priority.

**Scope is the most independent lens.** Evidence facets are visible through Logic and Scope. Logic facets are visible through Evidence. Scope facets are visible through Logic and Evidence. But visibility flows *toward* Logic and Evidence and *away from* Scope — almost no facet from Logic or Evidence is visible through Scope (source diversity is the exception). This means students looking through Scope will find Scope facets, but students looking through the other two lenses will rarely stumble onto Scope observations accidentally. The design implication: if you want students to develop Scope perception, you need to actively direct attention to the Scope lens rather than relying on cross-lens discovery. Session designers should ensure the Scope lens is explicitly prompted, not left as an afterthought after Logic and Evidence.

## Explanatory Variable Coverage

Every facet should be accountable by at least one cognitive pattern and at least one social dynamic, otherwise the explanation layer has a gap. These are common co-occurrences for pipeline design — illustrative, not exhaustive. Students may offer valid explanations not in these tables, and those explanations should be recognized as such.

### Cognitive Pattern Coverage

| Cognitive Pattern | Facets It Commonly Explains |
|---|---|
| Confirmation bias | Source diversity, relevance, perspective breadth, counter-argument engagement |
| Tunnel vision | Source diversity, internal consistency, consequence consideration, condition sensitivity |
| Overgeneralization | Relevance, sufficiency, condition sensitivity |
| False cause | Inferential validity |
| Uncritical acceptance | Source credibility, reasoning completeness |
| Black-and-white thinking | Inferential validity, consequence consideration |
| Egocentric thinking | Perspective breadth, condition sensitivity |
| False certainty | Sufficiency, reasoning completeness, condition sensitivity |

### Social Dynamic Coverage

| Social Dynamic | Facets It Commonly Explains |
|---|---|
| Conformity | Sufficiency, internal consistency, perspective breadth |
| Conflict avoidance | Perspective breadth, counter-argument engagement, internal consistency |
| Authority deference | Sufficiency, reasoning completeness, counter-argument engagement, source credibility |
| Groupthink | Source diversity, consequence consideration, condition sensitivity, counter-argument engagement |

**Gaps and notes:**

- **Egocentric thinking** connects primarily to perspective breadth. The connection to condition sensitivity ("only considering conditions relevant to people like yourself") extends its reach but it remains a narrow pattern. This is acceptable — not every pattern needs broad coverage.
- **Groupthink** connects to four facets across Scope and Evidence, reflecting its nature as a compound dynamic that degrades the group's willingness to stress-test anything. This makes it a rich explanatory variable for discussion.
- **False cause** connects only to inferential validity (where causal errors now live after the merge). This is correct — false cause is a specific inferential error, not a broad reasoning tendency.

## Priority Tiers for Pilot

Not every facet needs to appear in every scenario. For the 10-week UMS pilot, facets are organized into tiers based on cross-lens visibility (produces more perspectival diversity) and observability for 6th graders (lower floor for entry).

| Tier | Facets | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Core** (use from week 1) | Source credibility, relevance, sufficiency, inferential validity, perspective breadth, counter-argument engagement | High cross-lens visibility, accessible to 6th graders, produce rich discussion |
| **Extend** (introduce mid-pilot) | Source diversity, reasoning completeness, consequence consideration, condition sensitivity | Require slightly more sophisticated observation, best introduced after students are comfortable with lenses |
| **Reserve** (future iterations) | Internal consistency | Hardest to design for (requires characters contradicting themselves naturally), lowest cross-lens visibility, can be added in future iterations |

The pipeline only needs to design for the tier being used, and the evaluator only assesses against targeted facets. A facet in a lower tier may still surface naturally in a scenario — if a character contradicts themselves and students notice, the evaluator should recognize the observation even if internal consistency wasn't a design target.
